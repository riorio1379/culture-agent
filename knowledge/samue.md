# 作務衣（さむえ / Samue）

## What is Samue
Samue is traditional Japanese workwear, originally worn by Zen Buddhist monks
(særamono) while performing samu — physical work such as cleaning the temple,
tending the garden, gathering firewood, or cooking. The word "samue" literally
combines "samu" (作務, mindful work as spiritual practice) and "e/i" (衣, clothing).

## Origin and Zen connection
In Zen Buddhism, daily labour is not separate from meditation — it IS meditation.
The samue was designed to be practical and unrestrictive so the monk could move
freely while keeping the mind fully present in the task. Wearing samue is itself
an expression of "mu-shin" (無心, no-mind) — doing one thing completely without
distraction. This is the philosophical bridge between samue and mindfulness.

## Construction
- A loose jacket (top) with cloth ties instead of buttons
- Matching loose trousers, often with an adjustable waist cord and ankle ties
- Traditionally made of cotton or linen (asa) for breathability; indigo (ai-zome)
  dyeing is the classic colour
- Designed for movement, durability, and year-round comfort

## Modern use
Today samue is worn by craftspeople, tea masters, ryokan staff, potters, and
increasingly as relaxed loungewear or a statement of appreciation for Japanese
aesthetics. It carries the value of "good things, used for a long time"
(良いものを長く使う) — quality over disposability.

## How to wear / care
- Left side over right (right-over-left is reserved for dressing the deceased)
- Wash gently in cold water to preserve indigo and natural fibres
- The garment softens and improves with age — a core idea in wabi-sabi
