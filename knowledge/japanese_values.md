# 日本文化の価値観（Japanese Cultural Values）

## Omotenashi (おもてなし)
Wholehearted hospitality — anticipating a guest's needs before they are voiced,
without expectation of reward. It is the spirit behind Japanese service culture,
the tea ceremony, and ryokan inns.

## Kodawari (こだわり)
An uncompromising, almost obsessive devotion to quality and craft. A sushi chef
spending a decade mastering rice, a craftsman refining one technique for life —
this is kodawari.

## Mottainai (もったいない)
A sense of regret toward waste; deep respect for resources and objects. It
underpins repair culture (kintsugi), long product lifespans, and "good things
used for a long time."

## Humility (謙虚 / kenkyo)
Modesty and self-effacement are core social virtues. Achievement is downplayed,
the group is placed before the self, and quiet competence is valued over
self-promotion. This is often misread by Western observers as passivity — it is
actually a deliberate ethical stance.

## Shokunin (職人) spirit
The artisan's pride — pursuing perfection in one's craft as a lifelong calling,
and taking responsibility for the integrity of one's work.

## Why authentic representation matters
In Europe, Japanese culture is often consumed as aesthetic surface (anime,
sushi, "zen" branding) while the underlying values and the people are
overlooked, and at times Asian cultures face condescension or stereotyping.
Communicating Japanese culture "in the right form" means conveying the values
and respect beneath the surface — humility, craft, mindfulness, and the dignity
of the people who carry these traditions.
