# 禅とマインドフルネス（Zen & Mindfulness）

## Zen (禅)
Zen is a school of Mahayana Buddhism that emphasises direct experience and
seated meditation (zazen) over scripture and doctrine. It reached Japan from
China (where it is called Chan) and deeply shaped Japanese aesthetics, the tea
ceremony, calligraphy, garden design, and martial arts.

## Key concepts
- **Zazen (座禅)**: seated meditation; the core practice of Zen
- **Mu-shin (無心)**: "no-mind" — acting without the interference of ego or
  overthinking; full presence in the moment
- **Samu (作務)**: mindful physical work as a form of meditation
- **Ma (間)**: the meaningful use of empty space and pause
- **Ichigo ichie (一期一会)**: "one time, one meeting" — treasure each
  encounter as unrepeatable

## Wabi-sabi (侘び寂び)
The aesthetic of finding beauty in imperfection, impermanence, and simplicity.
A cracked tea bowl repaired with gold (kintsugi) is more beautiful for its
history, not less. This is the opposite of disposability culture.

## Zen and mindfulness in the West
Modern "mindfulness" practice in Europe and the US is largely secularised Zen
and Vipassana meditation. When introducing Japanese culture to a Western
audience, the bridge of mindfulness is powerful — but it should be presented
with respect for its Buddhist roots, not stripped of context. Authentic
representation means crediting the source and avoiding shallow "exotic"
stereotypes.

## Common misconceptions to correct
- Zen is not "emptying the mind of all thought" — it is full, non-judgemental
  awareness.
- Japanese minimalism is not merely a design trend; it grows from a spiritual
  and ethical worldview.
- "Eastern wisdom" should not be flattened into a single aesthetic — Japan,
  China, Korea, and other Asian cultures are distinct.
